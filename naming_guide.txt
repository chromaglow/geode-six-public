SECTION 2
The Formula — Five Simple Pieces
Every file we create gets a name built from five pieces, always in the same order, always separatedby underscores (
_
). No spaces. No slashes. No random abbreviations you'll forget in two weeks.
THE UNIVERSAL NAMING FORMULA
[PROJECT] _ [TYPE] _ [Description] _ [YYYYMMDD] _v[X.Y]
Example: PR1_TSR_EconomicDev_20260313_v2.0.docx
💡
Why underscores and not spaces?
Spaces in file names cpr5e technical problems in many systems — they get replaced withweird characters like
%20
in web links, and can break automated tools. Underscores look cleanand work everywhere.

SECTION 6
The Date Format — Why We Write It "Backwards"
This is the part that surprises people at first. We write dates as
YYYYMMDD
— year first, then month,then day. So March 13, 2026 becomes
20260313
.
🤔
Why not just write "Mar13" or "3/13/26" like normal?
Becpr5e when you sort files by name, "normal" dates don't sort correctly. You'd get Marchbefore February. You'd get 2025 mixed in with 2026. It becomes chaos the moment you havemore than a handful of files.
When you use YYYYMMDD, something magical happens. Sort any folder alphabetically, and everyfile instantly appears in
perfect chronological order
— no manual sorting, no confusion. It's one ofthe oldest tricks in file management and it works flawlessly.
✨
The self-sorting superpower
Agendas sorted by date. Meeting summaries in order. Draft history from first to latest —automatically. That's the payoff for writing the year first.
What date do you use?
For meeting documents (agendas, summaries), use the date of the meeting.For other documents, use the date you first created or finalized it.

SECTION 7
Version Control — Never Lose Track of "Which One Is Current"
Version control is just a way of keeping track of how a document has evolved over time. Instead ofrenaming files
FINAL
,
FINAL2
,
ACTUAL_FINAL
— we use a simple numbering system that everyoneunderstands instantly.
The Two-Number System
Every version looks like
vX.Y
— for example
v1.3
. Here's what X and Y mean:
v0.1 · v0.2 · v0.3
Draft.
You're still working on it. Not ready to share externally.
v1.0
First published version.
Ready — you'd be comfortable sending this to a partner or client.
v1.1 · v1.2 · v1.3
Minor updates.
Small edits or corrections to a published doc. Structurehasn't changed.
v2.0 · v3.0
Major revision.
The document has been substantially restructured or rebuilt.
📌
The golden rule: never delete old versions.
Keep both
v1.0
and
v2.0
in the folder. Old versions are your history — they let you go back,compare, or recover something that got cut. Storage is cheap. Regret is expensive.

SECTION 10
The Five Rules to Remember
Rule 1 — Always use the formula.
[PROJECT]_[TYPE]_[Description]_[YYYYMMDD]_v[X.Y]
— every time, no exceptions.Even for "quick" files. Especially for those.
Rule 2 — No spaces, ever.
Use underscores between pieces. Use CamelCase inside a description (e.g.,
EconomicDev
not
economic dev
).
Rule 3 — Dates go YYYYMMDD.
Year, then month, then day. Always. This is what makes files sort themselves automatically.
Rule 4 — Drafts start at v0.1, published docs start at v1.0.
If you'd feel comfortable sending it to a client, it's at least v1.0. If it's still a work in progress, it'sv0.x.
Rule 5 — Never overwrite. Always save a new version.
Instead of saving over the old file, save a new copy with the updated version number. Keepevery version. You'll thank yourself later.
